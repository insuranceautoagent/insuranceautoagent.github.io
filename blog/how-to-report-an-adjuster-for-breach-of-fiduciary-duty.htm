How to Report an Adjuster for Breach of Fiduciary Duty

Home / Blog / Blog Details

The relationship between an insurance policyholder and their claims adjuster is built on a foundation of trust, but it’s a specific kind of trust—a fiduciary duty. In an era defined by climate-driven mega-disasters, global supply chain fractures, and sophisticated cyber threats, the stakes of an insurance claim have never been higher. For families rebuilding after a wildfire, a business recovering from a ransomware attack, or a community flooded by a historic storm, the adjuster is the gatekeeper to financial survival. Yet, what happens when that gatekeeper betrays their fundamental duty? Understanding what a fiduciary breach looks like in today’s complex claim environment and knowing how to formally report it is not just about one claim; it’s about upholding the integrity of a system we all depend on in times of crisis.

A fiduciary duty is the highest standard of care in equity or law. For insurance adjusters—particularly those employed by or contracted to your insurance company—this duty is not merely about processing paperwork. It encompasses the obligations of utmost good faith, fair dealing, loyalty, and full disclosure to you, the policyholder. The adjuster must place your interests in receiving the full benefits owed under the policy at least on par with the interests of the insurance company. This is the bedrock of the relationship. When an adjuster prioritizes the insurer’s bottom line over your rightful claim, engages in deceptive practices, or willfully ignores policy language to underpay, they are not just being difficult; they may be violating their fiduciary duty.

The Modern Faces of a Breach: From Algorithmic Bias to Bad Faith

In today’s digital and disaster-prone world, breaches of fiduciary duty can take both classic and novel forms. Recognizing them is the first step.

1. The Algorithmic Shortcut

A pressing contemporary issue is the over-reliance on—or manipulation of—claims software and AI tools. Adjusters may use computer-aided estimating programs that systematically undervalue regional labor costs or omit necessary line items for complex repairs. When an adjuster knowingly uses a flawed output, refuses to manually adjust for clear discrepancies, or hides behind the "computer says no" defense without independent judgment, they fail their duty of care and disclosure. This is especially pernicious in claims involving new construction materials or green energy upgrades, where software may be outdated.

2. The Climate Catastrophe Playbook

Following widespread disasters like hurricanes, floods, and wildfires, some insurers engage in a pattern of tactics designed to minimize aggregate payouts. An adjuster acting under such directives might: * Unreasonably attribute damage to an excluded peril (e.g., claiming flood damage was actually "pre-existing wear and tear" in a hurricane claim where both wind and water are factors). * Impose oppressive documentation requirements beyond the policy terms, knowing records may have been destroyed in the event. * Delay, delay, delay. In a fiduciary breach context, unjustified delay is not just frustration; it’s a tactic that pressures desperate policyholders into accepting lowball settlements.

3. The Cyber Claim Muddle

Cyber insurance is a rapidly evolving field. An adjuster lacking expertise may breach their duty by misinterpreting the scope of business interruption coverage following an attack or by failing to properly advocate for the costs of forensic investigation, data recovery, and regulatory compliance. Loyalty to the policyholder requires the adjuster to seek out expert opinions when a claim is beyond their ken, not to deny based on ignorance.

4. The Silent Conflict of Interest

A clear breach occurs when an adjuster has an undisclosed financial incentive to close your claim for less. This could be a direct bonus structure, pressure from a third-party administrator (TPA) contract, or an informal quota system. The duty of loyalty demands transparency about any influences that might conflict with your best interest.

Building Your Case: Documentation is Your Shield

Before you report, you must build a compelling, factual record. Emotion, while valid, is not evidence. Your documentation must tell a clear story of the breach. * The Policy & Communications: Keep every single piece of paper and email. Your policy, all claim correspondence, adjuster notes, and settlement offers are core evidence. * The Discrepancy Log: Meticulously document each point of contention. For example: "On [Date], Adjuster Smith stated the roof required only repair, not replacement. Independent contractor [Name]'s estimate #123, attached, confirms full replacement is required per building code. Adjuster dismissed this without explanation." * Record Interactions (Where Legal): Check your state's consent laws for recording phone calls. If one-party consent is allowed, consider recording crucial conversations. Always inform the adjuster you will be taking detailed notes during calls. * Secure Independent Assessments: Hire licensed, reputable contractors, engineers, or content specialists. Their reports can objectively contradict a low or faulty adjuster’s estimate and demonstrate the adjuster’s failure to properly investigate. * Demand Explanations in Writing: Politely but firmly ask the adjuster to justify, in writing, specific denials or reductions, citing the exact policy language. Their inability or refusal to do so can be telling.

The Reporting Ladder: Where to File Your Complaint

Once armed with evidence, you must escalate through the proper channels. Start specific and move broader.

Step 1: The Insurance Company Itself

Formally report the adjuster to the insurance carrier’s internal compliance department, special investigation unit (SIU), or office of the CEO. Use a clear subject line: "Formal Complaint: Breach of Fiduciary Duty by Adjuster [Name], Claim #XXXX." Outline the facts chronologically, attach your evidence, and explicitly state you believe the adjuster has violated their fiduciary duty. This puts the carrier on direct notice and may trigger an internal review.

Step 2: The State Department of Insurance (DOI)

This is one of your most powerful tools. Every state has a DOI that regulates insurers and licensed adjusters. File a detailed, written complaint with your state’s DOI. They have the authority to investigate, audit the company’s practices, fine insurers, and even suspend or revoke an adjuster’s license. A DOI complaint carries significant weight and is often the catalyst for a serious re-evaluation of your claim.

Step 3: Professional Designation Bodies

If the adjuster holds a professional designation like CPCU (Chartered Property Casualty Underwriter), AAI (Accredited Adviser in Insurance), or SCLA (Senior Claims Law Associate), report the conduct to the granting organization (e.g., The Institutes, IIA). These bodies enforce codes of ethics and can censure, suspend, or revoke designations for unethical conduct.

Step 4: Legal Counsel & Civil Action

Consult with an attorney experienced in insurance bad faith litigation. A breach of fiduciary duty is a serious legal claim that may exist alongside a bad faith claim. An attorney can send a powerful demand letter and, if necessary, file a lawsuit. In many jurisdictions, a successful bad faith or breach of fiduciary duty claim can result in the recovery of not just the claim amount, but also consequential damages, emotional distress damages, and attorney’s fees.

Navigating the Aftermath: Protecting Your Recovery

The reporting process can be arduous. Maintain a business-like demeanor in all communications. Stick to the facts you have documented. Be persistent and follow up regularly, but avoid emotional threats. Remember, your goal is to get your claim paid correctly and to ensure the adjuster is held accountable so they cannot harm other vulnerable policyholders. Your action, while demanding, serves a broader purpose: it reinforces that the fiduciary duty is not a relic but a living, enforceable obligation essential to justice in our risk-filled world. The contract of insurance is a contract of peace of mind; reporting its violators is how we defend that peace for everyone.
